CHALLENGE

The iconic Tkts booth in the center of Times Square offers tourists, locals, and theater-lovers the opportunity to purchase discounted tickets to shows in New York City. Purchasing same-day Broadway and Off-Broadway tickets at lower prices helps make performances more accessible to the public, increases viewership at shows, and is an exciting way to spend an afternoon or evening in NYC.
We want to digitize the Tkts experience. The unpredictability of the dayâ€™s available tickets, the wait in line, and finally getting to see a Broadway show at an affordable price combine to define the Tkts experience.


BACKGROUND RESEARCH


USER NEEDS

Users of this app are currently unhappy that it does not offer the ability to search tickets, or see what shows are available. Reviews on the App Store confirm that the intention and purpose of the app needs to be reconsidered.


The following user needs were defined:

  1. Purchase tickets in the app
  2. View an updated list of available tickets
  3. View and display purchased tickets within the app
  4. Browse shows by genre and location
  5. Get accurate directions to theaters

SUCCESS METRICS

Based on user research and competitor analysis, the following criteria will be used to assess the success of the app redesign:

  1. The app will be used to sell same-day tickets.
  2. Users will be able to join a virtual queue to purchase tickets.
  3. Users will be notified as tickets sell out for specific shows as they wait in the queue.
  4. Users can login to an account to save purchase information.
  5. Users can access digital tickets in the app.

IDEATION

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

When creating the information architecture for this app, I had to consider how the different elements of the site were related and how they fit in the overall structure of the application.



USER FLOWS

Once the Information Architecture was created, I was able to construct user flows to demonstrate how and why a user would navigate through the application. In creating these user flows, it was important to include not only the different elements users would be interacting with, but also the questions and choices they would make that would lead them to different parts of the app. Each user flow began with a goal and demonstrated how the user would navigate through the application to reach their goal.
